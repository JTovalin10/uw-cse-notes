# CSE 311: Tautologies, Contradictions, and Contingencies

These three categories classify every proposition by *how often* it is true across all assignments of its variables.

#Definition A **tautology** is a proposition that is always true, regardless of the truth values of its variables.

#Definition A **contradiction** is a proposition that is always false, regardless of the truth values of its variables.

#Definition A **contingency** is a proposition that is neither a tautology nor a contradiction — it can be true or false depending on the variable values.

Use [[CSE311/Part I - Mathematical Foundations/Logic/Truth Tables|truth tables]] to classify any proposition: a tautology has T in every row, a contradiction has F in every row, and a contingency has a mix. This connects to [[CSE311/Definitions/Satisfiability|satisfiability]] — a tautology is satisfiable on every row, while a contradiction is unsatisfiable.
